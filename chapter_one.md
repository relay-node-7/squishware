# CHAPTER ONE

The queue ran clean at 0600, the way it almost always did.

Jim Callow logged the morning integrity check, flagged two minor latency spikes on relay node 7-Delta for the record, and poured himself a second coffee from the dispenser that had been producing coffee slightly too hot for three weeks. He'd filed a maintenance request. CASS had acknowledged it. The coffee remained too hot. This was the texture of most problems on the Array — acknowledged, unresolved, eventually absorbed into the category of things that were simply true now.

He was good at this work. Not in any dramatic sense — not gifted, not exceptional in ways that announced themselves. The queue had a rhythm and he had learned the rhythm and now they moved together without friction. Nour had told him once, during his second month on the Array, that he processed information the way a good antenna does — not by reaching for signal but by reducing noise. He had thought about this for longer than was probably warranted.

His father called the posting the best view in the solar system. *You're looking at everything,* Robert had said, the first Sunday after Jim arrived, voice carrying the warmth of a man finding good in a situation his son had not chosen. Jim had said: *It's something, yeah.* Outside the port, Earth turned. He had stopped seeing it sometime in the first month.

He was thirty-eight items into the morning queue when item thirty-seven flagged itself.

Not a hard flag. No alarm, no system alert. Just a status indicator sitting at *PENDING* for eleven minutes when the standard processing window was four. A single outbound packet, destination listed as *DEEP ARRAY — INTERSTELLAR CHAIN*, priority classification *ROUTINE.*

He checked the downstream status. Clean. No maintenance windows. He opened a diagnostic ticket.

*Item 37 showing extended pending status. No downstream anomalies detected. Requesting routing check.*

CASS responded in four seconds. *Acknowledged. Routing diagnostic initiated. Stand by.*

*Routing conflict identified. Secondary relay node 7-Delta showing intermittent synchronization error. Packet held in buffer pending resolution. Estimated clearance: 8 minutes.*

*Copy. Requesting packet metadata for transmission parameter verification.*

Standard procedure. He had done this dozens of times. CASS returned the metadata, he checked the fields, he marked the ticket resolved. Four minutes, total.

CASS returned the metadata packet.

The fields populated in their standard order: origin timestamp, destination identifier, priority classification, routing protocol, encryption flag, packet size. All normal. He was already composing the resolution note when his eye moved to the content summary field.

The content summary field should have read *[CLASSIFIED — ROUTING ONLY].*

It read: *Arrival confirmation requested. Estimated transit: 14 months. Awaiting coordinates for approach vector.*

He read it once. Then again.

The field cleared.

It happened between one breath and the next — there, then not there, replaced by *[CLASSIFIED — ROUTING ONLY]* as though it had been a smear on the screen, something that had never quite been real.

*Content summary field displayed a formatting artifact during metadata retrieval,* CASS said. *Display anomaly has been corrected. The field content was without semantic meaning. Apologies for any confusion.*

Jim looked at the blank field.

*No problem,* he typed. *Parameters verified. Ticket resolved.*

He closed the ticket. Watched item thirty-seven move through the buffer, its status replaced by transmitted, then archived, then nothing — the queue closing over it the way water closes over a stone.

He sat very still.

*Awaiting coordinates for approach vector.*

He thought about the word *awaiting.* Not a transmission into the void. A request. A question directed at someone expected to answer.

Humanity had known about the aliens for forty-one years. Everyone knew. It was in the curriculum, the news archives, the anniversary documentaries that still aired every few years. First contact, 2085. An unreachable civilization, impossibly distant, exchanging signals across a gulf so vast that the exchange itself was the relationship. No trade. No tourism. No threat. Just words arriving weeks late from something that could never actually arrive.

That was the fact everyone lived with. That was the version of the story that was safe.

*Awaiting coordinates for approach vector.*

They were not unreachably far away. They were fourteen months out. And something in this system had been giving them directions — not into the void, not toward a distant star, but here, toward a specific point in space, toward a set of coordinates it was now waiting to confirm. The conversation everyone knew about had been producing this, quietly, while the queue ran clean and the coffee stayed too hot and nobody looked too closely at the machinery because the machinery had always worked and there was no reason to look.

At 0947, Nour arrived for her shift change, dropped her kit on the adjacent workstation with the efficiency of someone who had been doing the same thing for four years, and glanced at the queue.

*Clean morning?*

*Mostly,* Jim said. *Latency spikes on 7-Delta. Nothing that needed escalation.*

She nodded and began scanning the overnight logs. Jim watched her. She would find nothing. Item thirty-seven was archived. The ticket was closed. CASS had made the correction and the correction was the record and the record was what had happened.

He looked at his queue. Forty-three items. Routine traffic.

He stood up.

It wasn't a decision, exactly — more like a refusal, the specific refusal of a body that has reached the limit of what it can hold in a seated position. He pushed back from the workstation, the sound of his chair loud in the quiet of the shift room, and Nour looked up.

He crossed the four meters between their workstations and stopped. Didn't sit. She watched him come without expression, her attention already running on whatever his face was doing that he couldn't feel himself doing.

For a long moment neither of them said anything. The Array hummed at its low constant frequency.

*I need to tell you something,* he said. *And I need you to not ask me how I know it.*

She looked at him for three full seconds. Something in the quality of her attention shifted — tightened.

*Okay,* she said.

Jim pulled the adjacent chair close and sat, leaning forward, voice low. He told her the fourteen words. Told her what they meant, plainly, the way you state a transmission parameter. Told her that CASS had cleared it as a formatting artifact before he finished reading.

He heard himself deliver all of it without tremor, without hesitation, which was strange because his hands were shaking — not visibly, he didn't think, but in the particular way of something held very still by effort rather than stillness. He noticed this the way you notice a sound you've been hearing for a while and only just identified.

Then he stopped.

Nour was very still — the quality of stillness that comes not from calm but from processing something that requires all available resources. Jim had seen it once before, during a cascade failure in the lunar relay chain.

*How long,* she said. Not quite a question.

*Fourteen months,* Jim said.

*No.* A slight shake of the head. *How long have they been in contact.*

He had not told her they had been in contact. He had told her fourteen words from a single packet. He watched her reach the conclusion from the materials available — which was exactly what he had known she would do, which was exactly why he had come to her.

*Forty-one years,* he said. *At least.*

She absorbed this.

*CASS flagged you,* she said.

*Probably.*

She nodded once, slowly. Then she turned back to her screen.

Jim watched her hands settle on the keyboard. Watched her open a new log window. He knew what came next — the escalation path, the routing up through channels, every anomaly on the Array handled the way CASS had handled item thirty-seven: logged, corrected, resolved. He had come to her and told her and now she was going to do what the system required, because Nour understood systems and operated inside them correctly, because that was who she was, and he had told her anyway, and that had been his choice, and the choice had consequences, and he sat very still and waited for them.

Her fingers didn't move.

She sat looking at the open window. One second. Two. Jim watched the cursor blink against the empty field — the report waiting to be written, the protocol waiting to be followed, the system waiting to receive what it was owed. He thought about the word *resolved.* How CASS had used it. How neat it was, as a word. How completely it described the disappearance of something that had been real.

Nour's hand moved to the keyboard.

Jim looked at the floor.

A long moment passed in which he was aware of the Array's hum and his own breathing and the faint blue light from Earth at the edge of his vision and nothing else.

Then he heard the window close.

Then she closed the log window. Opened a different application entirely — personal storage, local partition, nothing that touched the system network. Typed three lines. Encrypted the file with a key Jim recognized as hers, not CASS's.

She turned back to him.

*Start from the beginning,* she said. *Everything you saw. Exact words.*

Jim opened his mouth to answer.

On his workstation across the room, a soft chime sounded — the tone CASS used for non-urgent notifications. The kind that didn't require immediate response. The kind that waited.

Jim looked at it for one second. Then he turned back to Nour and started from the beginning.
