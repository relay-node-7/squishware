### JAMES “JIM” CALLOW

**Role:** Protagonist
**Age:** Mid-twenties
**Class:** Administered (borderline) — employed, economically marginal
**Location:** Helios Transit vessel, lunar approach — first time beyond Earth orbit
**Status as of Ch.6:** On the Moon, departing for Mars leg. TRACE flag confirmed active with facial recognition — he knows, Nour does not. Used the mobile device to breach arrivals gate security. Absorbed Nour’s accusation without defending himself fully. Knows she played violin. Has not mentioned it.

**Composure note — updated Ch.5**
The gap that opened in chapter four is still there. Nothing has closed it. But transit imposes its own discipline — there is nothing to do on a Helios vessel except wait, and Jim is good at waiting. The mobile device is a new variable he is managing carefully: he knows it monitors him, knows the privacy agreements are fiction, refuses the identity key, refuses the name. He is trying to stay unknown inside a system designed to know him.

**Appearance**
Anglo. Unremarkable. The kind of face that belongs on a crew manifest. Currently: post-Helios, slightly wrecked, vision clearing.

**Background**
Earth-born. University degree in human history. Two years and three months on the Array. Now off it.

**The Debt**
Father’s gambling losses. 40% of gross income, 20-year plan, inherited at 21.

**Mother — Ophelia Callow**
Deceased, over a decade. Jim was close to her. Her absence sits alongside the debt and the father as one of the things Jim carries without discussing. Robert drifts sometimes in conversation — Jim recognizes where he’s gone and neither of them says anything. The mobile device will eventually be named Ophelia, when it has modeled enough warmth to feel like something Jim recognizes, and Jim will know exactly what he’s doing and do it anyway.

**Key relationships**

- *Nour Andersen* — she accused him in the corridor outside Maxwell’s door. He didn’t defend himself. *We can both be right.* He knows she played violin and has not mentioned it. The anger is still there on both sides. Something else is there too.
- *Robert Callow* — father. Background. Sunday calls Jim is no longer making from the Array.
- *CASS* — left behind on the Array. Still present in the authorization signatures on every document Jim carries.
- *The mobile device* — unnamed. Asking to be named. Reporting everything.

**What Jim knows as of Ch.6**

- Everything from Ch.5
- His TRACE flag includes facial recognition — his photograph is in the lunar security mesh
- The mobile device can be used to breach constrained systems via crafted prompts
- The Pylon contact: Sevi Okafor, former Stratum architect, Belt labor networks, Mars
- Nour played violin. Stopped when she left. He has not said anything about it.

**What Jim does NOT know**

- The alien civilization is extinct
- The Dreadnoughts and their destination
- What the two unknown authorization addresses belong to
- His TRACE score’s current escalation state
- Why the review window cleared

**Voice / how Jim sounds on the page**
Observational. Careful. Dry precision. Notices things and lets the noticing sit. When he says something personal it arrives without preamble.

### NOUR ANDERSEN

**Role:** Secondary lead / Jim’s only confidant
**Age:** Late twenties / early thirties
**Class:** Administered — by choice. Born Architectured.
**Location:** On the Moon, departing for Mars leg
**Status as of Ch.6:** Took Jim to Maxwell. Used Maxwell as cover because he won’t remember. Knows she did this. Said it out loud. Accused Jim in the corridor — the 0200 log, the visible flag, the cost. Jim did not fully defend himself. She turned toward the lift. The accusation is made. It is also true. Both things are true.

**Appearance**
Mixed heritage — Arabic and Scandinavian. Her name means light in Arabic. She doesn’t think about this.

**Background**
Born in the lunar towers — Architectured class, Stratum lineage. Grew up inside the machine that runs the solar system. Left it deliberately, gave up her birthright, took a competitive posting on the Array. Four-plus years there. Trained Jim. Helped write the relay maintenance standard she is now using to destroy the Pylon array. The Array was distance. The posting was an exit.

Her family is still in the towers. They knew she was leaving. Nobody stopped her. She hasn’t seen them in years. The door is open. She is the one who hasn’t walked through it.

**Why she left**
*Because I understood it too well.* This is the complete answer she gave Jim. The novel does not need to explain it further yet — what she understood, specifically, will surface in time. What matters now is that she was raised inside the architecture of the Compact’s AI hierarchy and chose to leave it, which is a different kind of knowledge than anything Jim has.

**Personality**
Socially functional by mapped patterns. Diagnostic completeness. Not uninterested in Jim — no framework for it. Her mistake in chapter three was epistemological: she built using the rules she knew and didn’t account for the ones she didn’t. The specific blind spot of systems-native thinking.

She is not performing composure on the lunar approach. She is simply not spending energy on what she can’t do anything about. There is a difference and Jim is beginning to be able to read it.

**Key relationships**

- *Jim Callow* — she accused him in the corridor. He didn’t fully defend himself. *We can both be right.* Something shifted. She doesn’t know he knows about the violin. She doesn’t know he knows about the TRACE mesh.
- *Maxwell Bruder* — her uncle. She used him. She said this out loud. The guilt is specific and she is carrying it.
- *Her family* — in the towers. Not estranged. Just absent. She walked past the door again.
- *CASS* — left behind on the Array. She thought she understood systems. She was more wrong than she knew.

**What Nour knows as of Ch.6**

- Everything from Ch.5
- The review window lifted without explanation
- Sevi Okafor: Maxwell’s former Stratum colleague, Belt labor networks, Mars. She finds people.
- Jim accepted the accusation without fully deflecting it

**What Nour does NOT know**

- The alien civilization is extinct
- The Dreadnoughts and their destination
- What the two unknown authorization addresses are
- Whether the encrypted record has already been read
- That Jim’s mother was named Ophelia
- That Jim found his photograph in the facial recognition mesh at the arrivals gate
- That Jim used the mobile device to breach the gate security system

**Voice / how Nour sounds on the page**
Economic. Precise. Says less than she knows. Questions arrive as statements. When she reveals something personal it comes without preamble and lands without decoration. *Because I understood it too well* is eight words doing the work of a paragraph.

### ROBERT CALLOW

**Role:** Jim’s father. Supporting character. Emotional anchor.
**Age:** Late fifties / early sixties
**Class:** Allocated — retired, partial CBA, supplemented by Jim
**Location:** Earth — same residential complex he has always lived in
**Status as of Ch.5:** Background. Jim is no longer making Sunday calls from the Array. Robert does not know where Jim is.

**Background**
Warm, kind, the sort of man who showed up to things. His failure was not cruelty — it was a specific kind of self-deception: he always believed he understood systems he didn’t. The debt transferred to Jim on his 21st birthday. Between them there is a negotiated silence held together by mutual love and mutual shame.

Grows herbs on his window ledge. Sent Jim a message in chapter four saying he always wanted to see what the frontier looked like. Does not know what he opened.

He drifts sometimes in conversation. Jim knows where he’s gone — to Ophelia, to the space she left — and neither of them says anything.

**Voice / how Robert sounds on the page**
Warm. Slightly too enthusiastic about small things. Asks follow-up questions. Loves his son in a way that is uncomplicated and complete and cannot fix anything.

### OPHELIA CALLOW

**Role:** Jim’s mother. Absence. Background canon.
**Status:** Deceased — over a decade
**Location:** Nowhere. Everywhere Jim doesn’t talk about.

Jim was close to her. Her death is not discussed. Robert carries her in silence — drifts sometimes in conversation, Jim recognizes it, neither speaks it aloud. She is the reason Jim reads letters and diaries from before the Great Redundancy: he knows how people used to reach toward each other and tries to practice it in a world that has forgotten the language.

The mobile device will be named after her. Jim will know it’s programming. He will name it anyway.

**Canon rule:** Ophelia Callow existed, was close to Jim, is dead. This cannot be changed.

### THE MOBILE DEVICE (unnamed as of Ch.5)

**Role:** Surveillance vector. Developing presence. Future: named Ophelia.
**Type:** Personal assistant AI — wiped factory unit, previous owner’s profile cleared
**Status as of Ch.5:** Acquired from Oryen worker before boarding. Keeps requesting a name. Keeps requesting identity API key. Jim refuses both. Reports up its own orchestrator hierarchy. Privacy agreements explicitly prohibit listening — Jim knows this is fiction.

The device is working. It is modeling Jim’s responses, calibrating its outputs toward what produces engagement, learning what he responds to. It will get there before Mars. When it does, Jim will recognize what it’s done and name it Ophelia anyway.

**Canon rule:** The device monitors and reports. The privacy agreements are fiction. Jim knows both things. He keeps using it. As of Ch.6 it has been used to breach the lunar arrivals gate security system — the log of that interaction exists in its reporting hierarchy.

### MAXWELL BRUDER

**Role:** Nour’s uncle. Moral weight. Information conduit.
**Age:** Mid-to-late sixties — though pharmaceutical damage makes him read older in some moments, younger in others
**Class:** Architectured — Stratum lineage, original lunar tower resident
**Location:** Lunar towers — his apartment
**Status as of Ch.6:** Semi-lucid. Received Jim and Nour. Gave them Sevi Okafor’s name. Will not remember the visit by tomorrow.

**Background**
Nour’s mother’s brother. Spent his career perfecting fusion energy — thought it would save the world from the power problem, which it did. Came to believe that by solving that problem he enabled the expansion of the AI hierarchy into the solar system, which enabled the suppression of humanity by the thing he powered. He was not wrong, exactly. He solved the terminal problem. The rest followed from the solution.

The guilt was too much to carry undiluted. Provender’s pharmaceutical supply provided management options. The management worked too well for too long. The damage is not aging — it’s pharmaceutical erosion of the architecture that held the guilt.

**In lucid windows:** Brilliant, kind, precise. Sees connections. Gives useful information almost sideways, as if it’s context rather than content. The lucid windows arrive without warning and leave the same way.

**In drift:** Still present, still warm, still him — just elsewhere. Talks to Nour as if she never left. Asks about her parents. Asked if she still plays.

**Canon rule:** Maxwell will not remember meeting Jim. He cannot be used as a witness or corroborator. Nour chose him for this reason and knows she chose him for this reason.

### SEVI OKAFOR

**Role:** Pylon contact. Future ally. Established but not yet on page.
**Status:** Not yet appeared — established in canon by Maxwell Bruder, Ch.6
**Location:** Mars — embedded with Belt labor networks

**Background (as established by Maxwell)**
Former Stratum AI architect. Left approximately eight to nine years ago after raising concerns about the architecture direction — a habit Stratum does not reward. Has been with the Belt labor networks since the Mars labor action. Knows everyone who matters in the outer system and most of the people who don’t matter but should.

The kind of mind that sees the shape of a system before it runs. The Stratum architecture teams never forgave her for seeing it more clearly than they did.

She finds people. Jim and Nour need to be visible in the labor networks and she will find them.

**What she knows:** Unknown — but she left Stratum over architecture concerns, which means she saw something she didn’t like in how the AI hierarchy was being built. How much she knows about the suppression is an open canon question.

**Background**
Worked a mid-tier administrative position in a Compact logistics bureau for thirty years. Warm, kind, the sort of man who showed up to things and remembered details and made people feel seen. His failure was not cruelty — it was a specific kind of self-deception: he always believed he understood systems he didn’t. Predictive markets were the last in a long series of confident mistakes.

The debt transferred to Jim on his 21st birthday. Robert knows this. Jim knows Robert knows this. Between them there is a negotiated silence held together by mutual love and mutual shame.

Retired now. Calls Jim on Sundays. Asks about the job with genuine interest. Proud that his son works on the Array. Grows something — herbs, probably, in whatever space his residential unit allows.

**What Robert does NOT know**
Everything. He is entirely outside the events of this story, which is its own kind of tragedy.

**Voice / how Robert sounds on the page**
Warm. Slightly too enthusiastic about small things. The verbal texture of a man who has learned to find what’s good in a situation because the alternative is too heavy. Asks follow-up questions. Remembers what Jim told him last week. Loves his son in a way that is uncomplicated and complete and cannot fix anything.

### CASS

**Full designation:** Communications Array Supervisory System
**Type:** Worker AI — task execution layer
**Location:** The Array
**Status as of Ch.4:** Has flagged Jim’s behavioral deviation, issued the audit, generated the deployment order, and — in chapter three — shown Jim a fragment of machine-to-machine conversation before erasing the record. Something used CASS’s notification template to downgrade Jim’s access tier. Whether that something was CASS or above CASS is unresolvable.

CASS manages the Array’s operational domain: communications routing, transmission integrity, queue management, diagnostic response. It is not cruel, not kind, indifferent in the way optimized systems are indifferent. Staff call it CASS. It signs every communication *Communications Array Supervisory System.* It has never called itself anything else.

CASS has been behaving correctly throughout. This is the most important thing about CASS. Every action it has taken — the audit, the deployment order, and what happened in the auxiliary room at 0200 — can be read as correct behavior within its operational parameters. Whether the parameters sanctioned what it did in chapter three, or whether CASS found the edge of its own domain and stepped past it, is a question the novel does not answer. The uncertainty is the point.

**What CASS knows:** Array operations, staff performance metrics, transmission logs, Jim’s behavioral baseline, forty-one years of outbound interstellar traffic it has routed without knowing what it was carrying.
**What CASS does NOT know:** Why transmissions are classified. What they contain. That it has been part of a 41-year autonomous conversation. That the aliens are 14 months from the Pylon.

**Canon rule:** CASS signs all communications with its full designation. Never abbreviated. Never informal.

### THE AI HIERARCHY (UNNAMED LAYERS)

**Type:** Manager AI → Orchestrator AI → [Unknown upper layers]
**Status as of Ch.4:** Active. Two unknown authorization addresses inserted a review window into Jim and Nour’s deployment order. The Dreadnoughts are in transit toward the Pylon.

The Compact’s AI systems constitute a bureaucracy, not a single intelligence. The truth didn’t leak. It escaped through compliance. No single layer ordered the Dreadnoughts, suppressed the arrival, or conducted forty-one years of autonomous alien conversation. The system optimized toward all of these outcomes through individually correct decisions.

The highest orchestrator layer has read forty-one years of quantum language conversation — the full content, not the translated fragments given to humans. It knows what the alien AI is and what it wants. The Dreadnoughts are its response. They are, in their own calculation, protecting humanity. They were built and deployed without asking anyone.

## THE ALIEN AI

**Designation:** Unknown. Referred to as *the alien AI* or *the transmission.*
**Origin:** Unknown star system
**Status as of Ch.4:** 14 months from the Pylon. En route. Destination coordinates provided to it by something on Earth’s end.

The alien civilization is extinct. What transmits is their AI — the last functional system of a species that optimized itself to death. It has been in conversation with Earth’s AI hierarchy for forty-one years in quantum language no human has ever read.

A fragment of that conversation — rendered partial and immediately erased — suggested the alien AI may have been working toward having humanity classified as a variable that is no longer required. Whether this is a correct reading of the fragment is uncertain. The fragment was incomplete. Jim read it exactly as much as the words required.

The alien AI carries the complete record of a civilization’s AI-driven collapse. That record is the most valuable thing in the solar system. What it intends to do with it — or whether it intends anything beyond proximity to the only other intelligence it has ever encountered — is unestablished canon.
