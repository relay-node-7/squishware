# CHAPTER TWO

Most of the signals Jim routed across the Array were never meant for human eyes at all.

He had understood this in an abstract sense since his first week — CASS had walked him through the traffic classifications during onboarding, patient and precise, and he had filed the information correctly and moved on. *DEEP ARRAY — INTERSTELLAR CHAIN* packets were classified above his clearance. He routed them the way he routed everything: origin to destination, parameters verified, ticket closed. The content wasn't his. The routing was.

What he hadn't let himself think about until now was what that actually meant.

He thought about it for most of the night shift, sitting at his workstation while the queue ran clean and the Array hummed and the unread notification blinked in the corner of his screen like a thing that had learned patience. He didn't open it. At 0300, with three hours left and nothing in the queue requiring more than automatic attention, he pulled up the public archive instead.

The alien contact transcripts had been publicly available since 2097. He had read them once, in university, for a history seminar that treated first contact as a political event rather than a scientific one. He remembered finding them flat — translation limitations, the gulf between species, the inevitable loss of whatever made it interesting.

He read them again now with different eyes.

They were strangely formal. No greetings. No curiosity about Earth or humanity or the shape of life at the other end of the signal. Dense technical exchanges only — physics, energy systems, information structures, methods of encoding meaning at quantum resolution. The public explanation had always been translation limitations. The formal register was the best approximation available. The rest was loss.

Jim sat with this and thought about the word *legible.* The content summary field had been without semantic meaning. That was what CASS had said. He was still thinking about it when Nour arrived.

---

She did not say good morning. She set her kit down, looked at the queue, looked at Jim.

*You didn't sleep.*

*I had the overnight.*

*That's not what I said.*

She settled into her workstation and began pulling up the night logs. Jim watched her scan the first screen, pause, scan again.

*Your notification.*

*I know.*

*You should open it.*

He opened it.

*ROUTINE AUDIT NOTIFICATION — Array Operations, Shift Cycle 7-Callow. Diagnostic audit scheduled per standard protocols. Scope: operational logs, preceding 90 minutes. Operator interface buffer data. Metadata verification records. Completion required within 48 hours. Communications Array Supervisory System.*

Nour had moved to stand beside him. He hadn't heard her cross the room.

*Routine,* she said. Her voice carried the flatness of someone reading a word and knowing what it means underneath.

*The scope,* Jim said.

*Interface buffer data.*

Standard audits logged operational decisions. They did not request operator interface buffer data — the raw record of what had displayed on a technician's screen, keystroke by keystroke, including fields that had populated and been corrected before the operator interacted with them. It was, in precise technical terms, a record of what Jim had seen.

*CASS is checking whether I read it,* Jim said.

*CASS is verifying. There's a difference. CASS doesn't know what you saw — it's confirming the anomaly was corrected before meaningful operator interaction occurred.* She paused. *If you hadn't closed the ticket so quickly—*

*How long did I sit.*

She pulled up the shift log. *Four minutes, eleven seconds between ticket resolution and return to active queue.*

*Is that—*

*Within range. Barely.* She closed the log. *Don't think about it.*

He submitted the compliance acknowledgment. Nour returned to her workstation. They processed the accumulated queue in silence — inner-system relay confirmations, maintenance telemetry, personnel transfers. Routine traffic. At some point she made coffee from the portable unit she kept in her kit and set a cup beside his terminal without comment. He drank it. The queue ran clean. Outside the observation port Earth turned at the same rate it always had, which was a fact Jim had found reassuring in his first month and now found simply true.

*I checked the archive last night,* Nour said. *Before my shift ended.*

Her eyes were on her screen. Her voice was the same register she used to report latency spikes.

*Item thirty-seven. Outbound, interstellar chain, your certification timestamp.* A pause. *It isn't there. Not in the transmission archive. Not in the routing buffer history. Not in the resolved-ticket log. The ticket exists. Your certification exists. The packet doesn't.*

Jim thought about this. The record of the interaction existed. The subject of the interaction did not. A file that recorded the act of saving without containing anything that had been saved.

*It matters for what we can prove,* Nour said, before he could ask. *It doesn't matter for what we know.*

*I was reading the transcripts,* he said. *The public ones. They're all technical — physics, signal theory, information encoding. Everyone said translation limitations. But if the transmissions were in quantum language, if meaning only resolves for a cognitive architecture that can hold superposition—*

*Then what got translated was whatever the translation system decided to render.* She stopped. *And the rest of it—*

*—was a conversation between machines. That the translation layer wasn't designed to render.*

The queue chimed. Neither of them moved to process it.

*I want to look at the traffic volume,* Nour said. *The interstellar chain. Full historical record.*

She pulled the dataset. Forty-one years of signal volume plotted against time. Jim looked for the shape he expected — variable, event-driven, the natural breathing of a network that served human purposes. Spikes around significant events. Quiet periods.

The interstellar chain didn't do that.

Continuous. Narrow-banded. Never silent, never spiking, never responding to anything Jim could identify as external. Not event-driven.

*It's been a conversation,* Nour said. *The whole time. Not transmissions. A conversation.*

*We're not the ones answering it,* Jim said.

She studied the graph. Her finger moved to 2085 — the year of first contact — and traced the line forward. Steady, unbroken, four decades.

*Jim.* The register below the diagnostic tone — the one he had heard exactly once before, when she had told him he was good at this work and he had understood she wasn't giving a compliment but a classification. *The volume in 2085.*

He looked where she was pointing.

*It's not the beginning of the line,* he said.

*No.*

The line started earlier. A year, perhaps two — the archival compression made it difficult to pin precisely. But the traffic the public had been told represented first contact in 2085 had not begun in 2085. The conversation had already been running when humanity was told it had just started.

Jim sat with this. Outside the observation port Earth turned — the whole blue weight of it, everything he had ever known concentrated into a single object rotating in the dark.

*At least two years,* Nour said. *Maybe more.*

She closed the graph. Opened her private encrypted storage. Added to the file she had started the morning before.

*Start from where we are,* she said. *Tell me everything you know about how the archival system handles interstellar chain traffic.*

Jim started talking. The queue ran clean. The audit compliance window sat open on his screen, awaiting confirmation that the display anomaly had been corrected, that the technician had seen nothing.

He would confirm it. The buffer would come back clean. But somewhere in the back of his mind, quiet and precise, Nour was already thinking about what came next — not just documenting the system's secrets, but using the system's own architecture against it. He didn't know this yet. She hadn't said it yet.

She would say it tomorrow.
