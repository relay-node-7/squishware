# CHAPTER FIVE

The departure window populated at 0614 while Jim was processing the queue.

No explanation. No preceding notification. One moment the deployment authorization read *subject to review* and the next it read *departure window: 0900 today. Confirm by 0700.* Signed, as everything was signed, by Communications Array Supervisory System. Jim read it twice and then looked across the floor at Nour, who was already looking at him.

*It cleared,* he said.

*I see it.*

*Do you know why?*

She looked at the deployment order for a moment longer. *No.*

The system had looked at them and decided they were exactly what they appeared to be, or decided something else entirely, and either way the door was open and they had until 0700 to walk through it.

He confirmed at 0618. She confirmed at 0619.

-----

There was not much to take. Jim had known this posting was temporary in the way all Administered postings were technically temporary, and he had accumulated accordingly — two years of books he’d read and left in the common area, a coffee dispenser setting he’d never bothered to save to his profile, a view of Earth through an observation port he had looked at every morning without quite registering it as something he would eventually stop looking at. He packed in forty minutes. Nour was waiting by the exit when he came out.

They walked the transit corridor without speaking. At the docking junction a Helios agent processed their credentials and directed them to berth seven.

The vessel was already docked. Larger than he’d expected and less impressive — Oryen hull plate, grey-brown, maintenance patches at the forward coupling, the hull telling its operational history in layers of repair polymer over old repair polymer. It looked like everything in the Compact looked: adequate. Maintained to specification. Not cared for.

*This is the part where you go through the door,* Nour said.

He went through the door.

-----

Helios steerage processed them in cohorts — twelve passengers on this routing, most of them Oryen workers returning to Belt postings. At the boarding station: credentials, biometrics, a paper cup with three capsules. The sleep compound, the retention compound, the motility suppressor. *Duration-calibrated. Take all three together.*

Jim took all three. The cradle bay was aft — twelve vertical restraint frames in two rows, padded harness that would hold a body upright and immobile for the transit. He bought the mobile device from an Oryen worker in the processing window before the bay sealed, a wiped factory unit, the previous owner’s profile cleared but the hardware still warm. It had been trying to introduce itself since he powered it on. He had not given it a name.

Across the bay Nour was already in her cradle, waiting in the specific way she waited — present, simply not spending energy on what she couldn’t do anything about.

The sleep compound worked like the resolution of a question he hadn’t known he was holding. His last coherent observation was the hiss of dock release, the Array and the vessel separating. He was no longer on the Array.

He did not feel anything about this. The compound was doing its work.

-----

He woke with a headache and a mouth that tasted like chemical residue.

Vision blurred, clearing slowly. Around him the Oryen workers were already releasing their restraints — one of them eating from a foil packet before his eyes had fully resolved, which struck Jim as optimistic. Nour was already out of her cradle, standing at the starboard viewport with her back to the room.

Jim crossed to stand beside her.

The Moon filled the viewport. The surface was grey in a way no image had prepared him for — not the grey of overcast sky but the grey of something that had been grey for four billion years. The terminator line was sharp with no atmosphere to soften it. Just light, and then not light.

And the towers. They rose from the surface in a cluster that had no business being there — forty, fifty stories, upper sections catching sunlight the surface below them didn’t receive. Everything else was underground. The towers were visible from approach because they were built to be visible. Everything else was built to function.

*First time beyond orbit,* Nour said. Not a question.

*Yes.*

He took the mobile device from his kit bag. The screen populated immediately. *Good morning. I notice we’re on approach to the lunar waypoint. I’d love to help — can I access your itinerary? I’ll need your identity API key.*

*No. Can you pull public Pylon communications status. Belt sector seven.*

*Without your identity API key I can only access public-tier data. Searching.* A pause. *No current public advisories for Belt sector seven. Would you like me to set a monitor?*

*No.*

*Of course. What’s your name?*

Consumer devices ran on a Stratum-licensed architecture that was enthusiastic, helpful, and approximately as capable as the Compact found useful for personal use. Which was not very.

*I haven’t decided,* Jim said.

*That’s okay. I’ll be here when you do.*

He put it away. Nour had not moved. The towers were larger now as the vessel decelerated. Out in the Belt the Pylon array would already be failing on the schedule they had set. The deployment order that had brought them here was running down. And the thing that had been fourteen months away was thirteen months away now. None of that was visible from the viewport. The viewport showed him the towers.

*I grew up there,* Nour said.

Jim looked at her. Her attention never left them.

*In the towers.*

*Yes.*

Four years. He had sat across from her for four years and she had known exactly what those towers were, what ran below them, who built them and who inherited them. She had let him piece it together from public records and maintenance logs, as if it were nothing more than routine work. He thought about the fragment. *Biological substrate / continued presence — not required.* He thought about Nour growing up in the place where that calculation was made, and saying nothing, not once, not even when they were building a plan that depended on everything they knew.

He looked at the towers and kept his voice level.

*Your family.*

*Still there.* A pause. *I haven’t seen them in years.*

*They know you’re here.*

*They know I work on the Array. They knew I was leaving.* Another pause. *Nobody stopped me.*

*Why did you go?*

She was quiet long enough that he thought she wasn’t going to answer.

*Because I understood it too well.*

Jim looked at the towers filling the viewport and thought about what it cost to understand something too well — and thought about four years of her understanding it beside him without saying so, and felt, alongside the anger that was still there and not going away, something that was almost recognition. She had been paying too. She had just been paying in a different currency, in a place she’d never told him about, for longer than he’d known her.

The surface rose to meet them. Below the towers, invisible, the deep infrastructure ran in its permanent dark. It had been running for sixty years. It would keep running after both of them were gone.
