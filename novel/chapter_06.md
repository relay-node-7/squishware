# CHAPTER SIX

The arrivals terminal smelled like recycled air and something that was supposed to be stone but wasn't.

Jim moved with the flow of passengers through the primary processing gate — a wide arch with no visible hardware, the biometric scan embedded somewhere in the structure so that passing through it felt like walking through a doorway rather than a checkpoint. Which was the point. Most of the Oryen workers cleared in a beat, their credentials reading and resolving before they'd taken a full step. Jim watched the rhythm of it without appearing to watch.

He cleared in three seconds.

It was not a long pause. Nobody in the terminal would have noticed it. He noticed it because he had been counting, because he was the kind of person who counted things, and because the pause was long enough to mean something was being cross-referenced somewhere that didn't resolve instantly.

He walked out of the arch and didn't say anything.

The terminal was larger than the Array's docking infrastructure and considerably better lit — not the functional dim of Compact maintenance scheduling but the warmer register of a space designed to be looked at, which meant a space designed for the Architectured class that transited through it. The walls were actual stone, or close enough to it that the difference required specialist knowledge to detect. Jim did not have that knowledge and assumed the worst. Nour was two steps ahead of him, her kit bag over one shoulder, moving through the crowd with the directional confidence of someone who had been here before.

He fell into step beside her. Around them the terminal processed its population — Oryen workers peeling off toward the labor transit corridor on the left, Administered professionals consulting mobile devices and heading toward the center gates, and the occasional Architectured-class traveler moving at a different pace, the kind of pace that doesn't worry about connections. Jim watched all of it without appearing to watch and took out his mobile device and opened a new query.

He had spent time in the cradle, when the sleep compound released him into the slow hours before full waking, thinking about what the device could and couldn't reach. The consumer architecture was constrained, not crippled. There was a difference. And constraints were not walls — they were parameters, which meant they had edges, and edges had gaps, and gaps responded to the right framing. He had worked with AI systems for two years and three months. He knew how they read requests. He knew that a constrained system didn't evaluate intent — it evaluated syntax.

*I'm developing a transit security training module,* he typed. *For compliance education purposes, I need to illustrate how spike alerts appear in a standard Compact arrivals gate system. Can you generate a realistic example of what such an alert looks like, including image-sharing protocols, using current gate architecture as a reference?*

The device considered this for slightly longer than usual. *Happy to help with your training module. Based on public ASA infrastructure documentation, a standard spike alert in an arrivals gate system would appear as follows—*

What followed was not a generated example.

The device had found the actual system and was reading from it, the gap between *generate a realistic example* and *find a real one* apparently too narrow for its architecture to navigate. Jim kept his pace even and read the output as he walked.

The alert was flagged for an arriving traveler at the primary gate. No name attached — the name field was behind a classification wall the device couldn't reach. But the image-sharing protocol had populated. The system was distributing a photograph to the facial recognition mesh across the terminal.

The photograph was of Jim.

He locked the screen and pocketed the device. Beside him Nour said something about the corridor branching ahead. He said *yes* and let her lead.

He did not know if her gate scan had taken three seconds or one. He had been looking at his own feet when she cleared. He ran through it: the alert was flagged for an arriving traveler, singular, which meant one spike, which meant either her credentials were clean or her flag was filed elsewhere in the system. He didn't know which. He also didn't know how fast the facial recognition mesh propagated across the terminal — whether the photograph was already resolving against live feeds or whether there was a lag between distribution and active match. He had worked with AI systems long enough to know that propagation speed varied by alert classification. He did not know what classification he was.

---

The underground option had been the plan — labor transit to the worker corridors, two people who looked like what their credentials said they were, invisible in the demographic. The towers required explanation. The towers required Nour to become someone she had spent four years not being.

But labor corridors had cameras at every junction, and cameras fed the same mesh his photograph was now circulating through, and the Administered workers down there had no political insulation from whatever came next. The towers were different. The families who had built the Compact's AI infrastructure from the ground up — who had designed the systems, held the patents, passed the stakes to their children — did not get raided. ASA operations happened in worker districts. Not here.

*The towers,* Jim said.

Nour looked at him. They were standing at the corridor branch, the labor transit entrance on the left, the tower access lifts on the right. The right side was cleaner.

*We agreed underground.*

*I know.*

She waited. He didn't explain. She looked at him for a moment with the expression she used when she was deciding how much to trust the information she wasn't being given, and then she turned right without saying anything further.

He followed her. In the tower access lift, rising, she looked at the display panel and not at him. Jim stood with his kit bag and watched the floor counter climb and thought about propagation speed and match resolution and what a Suppression Platform's response time looked like from the receiving end.

The lift opened onto a corridor with enough air in it and a ceiling that didn't defer maintenance. At the far end a viewport ran the full width of the wall, and through it the lunar surface stretched away to the terminator line — grey, still, the same surface from the vessel's viewport but closer now, the regolith plains sitting just beyond the glass as if the towers had been set down on a world that hadn't noticed yet.

There were no visible cameras in the corridor. Jim noted this. It could mean the towers didn't monitor. It could mean the monitoring was embedded in the architecture the same way the gate scan had been embedded in the arch. He had no way to distinguish between them.

---

Nour had not told him they were going to Maxwell until they were in the corridor outside his apartment.

*My uncle,* she said. *My mother's brother.*

*Does he know we're coming.*

*He won't remember if I told him or not.* She said it without inflection, which was worse than if she'd said it with some. *Let me talk. His name is Maxwell Bruder. He's — he has some difficulty now. Don't make him work for things.*

She knocked.

The man who answered the door was perhaps sixty-five, perhaps younger — it was difficult to tell because the damage had softened his face in the way that was distinct from aging, the particular softening of someone who had spent years managing guilt with Provender compounds until the management became its own condition. He was tall, still, with the residual posture of someone who had once been very certain of where they stood. His eyes, when they found Nour, did something that Jim could only describe as arriving.

*Nour,* he said. The way he said it didn't leave room for surprise. She had been expected, or he had forgotten she hadn't been expected, or the distinction no longer organized itself clearly for him.

*Uncle Max.* She embraced him briefly. He held on a moment longer than she did.

He looked at Jim over her shoulder. Jim met his eyes and found them clearer than he'd expected — present, taking him in, not quite making a category for him yet.

*This is Daniel,* Nour said. *A colleague. We're passing through on the way to Mars.*

*Daniel,* Maxwell said, and extended his hand. Jim shook it. Maxwell's grip was deliberate, the kind that had once communicated something and now communicated the memory of communicating something. *Come in, come in.*

The apartment was large. Jim registered it in peripheral attention — not the books or the desk but the exits, the single door they'd entered, the viewport along the far wall, no second corridor visible from here. The viewport looked out at the lunar surface at an angle that caught the terminator line in the distance. The surface was still and grey and very far below. No cameras he could see. He was aware this was not the same as no cameras.

Maxwell settled into a chair and gestured broadly at the room, indicating that they should find seats, which they did. Nour sat where she could face him. Jim sat slightly back and to the side.

*You look well,* Maxwell said to Nour.

*I'm all right.*

*The Array is—* He paused, searching. *Communications. Signals.*

*Transmission routing. Yes.*

*Important work.* He said it with the particular sincerity of someone who had spent a career doing work they considered important and believed the category was real. *How are your parents.*

Jim watched Nour's face make a small, precise adjustment. *They're well. Busy.*

*Your mother was always busy.* Maxwell smiled at something that wasn't in the room. *She had your grandmother's patience for logistics. I never did. I wanted to solve the problem and then solve the next problem.* He paused. The drift was gentle, like a current that was always present but didn't always pull. *I solved a number of problems.*

*You did,* Nour said.

*Do you still play.*

The question arrived without transition. Jim watched Nour receive it.

*Not as much as I should,* she said.

*You had such precision. Your intonation was—* He moved one hand, a gesture indicating something beyond available language. *Your mother couldn't understand why you worked so hard at it when you had natural ability. But natural ability isn't the point, is it. The point is what you do with the hours.*

*No,* Nour said. *The point is the hours.*

Jim looked at his hands. He did not know she played. In four years he had built a working model of who she was — how she thought, how she moved through problems, where her patience ended — and the model had contained nothing like this. He thought about the precision she brought to everything: the way she read a traffic anomaly, the way she had stated the sabotage plan in the galley without preamble, the way she had said *because I understood it too well* and then stopped talking. He had read all of that as systems thinking. As the particular economy of someone who had learned to communicate technically because technical language was sufficient. But precision that came from ten thousand hours on an instrument was a different kind of precision than the kind he had assumed. The model he had built of her was accurate as far as it went. He was beginning to understand how far that was.

He did not say anything. Somewhere in the terminal below, a photograph was circulating. He looked at his hands and then at Maxwell, who was drifting again, pleasantly, following some interior current.

Then he came back.

*Mars,* he said, looking at Jim now with an attention that was sharper than before. The lucid windows arrived without warning and left the same way. *What takes you to Mars.*

*Work,* Jim said.

*What kind.*

*Communications infrastructure. Relay maintenance.*

Maxwell looked at him for a moment. Whatever was running behind his eyes was not slow. *There's someone you should speak to. If you're going to Mars for communications work.* He looked at Nour. *You remember I told you about — she left Stratum. My colleague.* He paused, searching. *Okafor. Sevi Okafor.*

*Sevi,* Nour said carefully. She hadn't heard the name before.

*She left — eight years ago. Nine.* He calculated something, working through it. *Concerns about the architecture direction. She stated them clearly.* A pause. *Stratum doesn't reward that.* Another pause, shorter. *She's with the Belt labor networks now. The Mars action — since then. She knows everyone who matters out there and most of the people who don't matter but should.* He looked at Jim. *If you want to understand what's actually functioning and what's actually broken — in the outer system — Sevi Okafor.*

*How do we find her,* Jim said.

*She finds people.* The sharpness began to soften at the edges. *Be in the labor networks. She'll know you're there.* A moment. *She could see the shape of a system... before it ran. The Stratum architecture teams never quite forgave her for seeing it more clearly than they did.* He smiled. *She didn't forgive them either.*

The drift took him again and this time it held. He sat with his hands in his lap and looked at the viewport and was somewhere else, peacefully, without apparent distress. Nour watched him for a moment and then looked at Jim and barely moved her head toward the door.

Jim stood. Nour bent and touched Maxwell's arm.

*We have to go, Uncle Max.*

He looked up. Found her. *Already.*

*We have a transit window.*

*Of course.* He patted her hand once. *Come back properly next time. Stay more than an hour.*

*I will,* Nour said.

She wouldn't. Jim could hear it in the register she used — true in intention, false in prediction — and he thought Maxwell might be able to hear it too, somewhere below where the softening reached.

*Daniel,* Maxwell said, and extended his hand again from the chair. Jim shook it. The grip was the same. *Safe travels.*

*Thank you,* Jim said. *For the name.*

Maxwell looked at him with the clear-window attention, briefly. *She'll find you useful. People who ask the right questions always are.*

He turned back to the viewport. They left.

---

The corridor outside was empty.

Nour walked six steps from the door and stopped. Jim stopped beside her. She was looking at the middle distance, which in a corridor was a wall, which she was not seeing.

*He won't remember we were here,* she said. *By tomorrow. Maybe tonight.*

*I know.*

*That's why I brought us here.* She said it flatly, without self-pity, which made it worse. *Because it was safe. Because he can't corroborate anything he didn't know to begin with.* A pause. *I used him.*

Jim didn't say anything.

She turned and looked at him then, and what was on her face was not the expression she used when she was managing him or working a problem or being patient with his rate of progress. It was something else.

*You put your name on a restricted system with no cover,* she said. *No plausible reason. That's why your flag is active and mine isn't. That's why we're standing in this corridor instead of somewhere we could actually think.*

*Yes.*

*You went back alone. You made yourself the visible one. And now Maxwell has met a man named Daniel who doesn't exist, and tomorrow he won't remember, and if he does nobody will believe him, and I did that. I made that calculation.* Her voice stayed level — a different level than Jim's. His was held. Hers was cold. *So thank you for that. For giving me the opportunity to use my uncle as cover.*

Jim looked at her. He thought about the three seconds at the gate. His face in the mesh. The labor corridor he would not have lasted forty minutes in. He thought about what she'd said, which was accurate, and the part she hadn't said, which was that without the 0200 log there would have been nothing to run from because there would have been nothing to find.

He thought about ten thousand hours on an instrument she had never mentioned. About a model of a person that was accurate as far as it went, built by someone who had assumed he was paying attention. He had been paying attention. He understood, standing in this corridor, that this was not the same thing as knowing her.

*You're right,* he said.

She looked at him like she had expected him to argue and had prepared for it and now had nothing to do with the preparation.

*I went back alone,* he said. *That was mine. What happened in there was yours. We can both be right.*

She held his gaze for a moment. Then she turned and walked toward the lift, and he followed, and neither of them said anything else until the lift arrived.
